# Lazy DM Prep Guide

A practical session prep framework for this Exandria campaign, distilled from Mike Shea’s *The Lazy Dungeon Master* (2012) and *Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master* (2018), and tailored to our table.

---

## Part 1: The Core Philosophy

### What “Lazy” Actually Means

“Lazy” does not mean unprepared. It means **preparing only what matters** and trusting yourself to improvise the rest. Most DMs over-prepare in the wrong areas (scripting dialogue, pre-plotting outcomes, building encounters the party never sees) and under-prepare in the areas that actually make sessions sing (understanding character motivations, having flexible secrets, knowing what’s interesting about the world right now).

The goal: **spend less time preparing and more time at the table being present with your players.**

### The Two Foundational Principles

**1. Prepare what you cannot easily improvise; skip what you can.**

You can improvise NPC names on the fly (or pull from a list). You cannot easily improvise the emotional throughline of a session or what secrets the players might uncover. Prepare the hard stuff. Let the easy stuff happen naturally.

**2. Let the players’ choices drive the story.**

Your prep should create a *situation*, not a *plot*. The players decide what happens. Your session notes from Sessions 1-7 already show this instinct -- you set up locations (warehouse, Sluiceweave, tavern) and let the party choose their path. Lean into that.

## Part 2: The Eight Steps of Lazy Prep

*Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master* refines the original book’s approach into eight concrete steps. Not every step is needed for every session. The first two are essential; the rest are used as needed.

### Step 1: Review the Characters (5 minutes)

**Always do this. It is the most important step.**

Before anything else, re-read your party’s current state. For our campaign:

- **Ada Glowsword** -- Cobalt Soul monk/researcher. Summoned something from the Book of True Names. Scholarly interests, ley line knowledge, connection to mentor Onde Orsal.
- **Alarise Stormshore** -- Matron of Ravens devotee. Holy quest to find the Matron’s body. Attacked Skrang. Trinket mystery. Strong moral convictions.
- **Helga Hirscka** -- Barbarian looking for love and adventure. Direct, physical problem-solver. Coffee enthusiast. Sushi the dog.
- **Lucien Sugea** -- Marquess’s son with unknown power source (U’kotoa connection?). Speaks Abyssal unknowingly. Caught between mother and party. Did NOT tell his mother about Dazz’s quest.
- **Shin Cherou** -- Priestess of Avandra, Lucien’s au pair. Protector, faith-driven. Family she supports financially. Threatened/bribed by the Marquess.

Ask yourself:

- What does each character **want** right now?
- What **unresolved tension** exists for each character?
- How can I **connect** this session’s events to at least two characters’ personal arcs?

### Step 2: Create a Strong Start (5 minutes)

**Always do this.**

Do not start with “so, what do you do?” Start with something happening. Drop the players into action, a decision, or a compelling scene.

A strong start is:

- A **situation** that demands a response
- Something with **stakes** the players care about
- Connected to what happened **last session**

**Template:** “We open on [character/location] as [something is happening]. [Sensory detail]. [Implied question or choice].”

**Examples from our campaign:**

- Session 7 started with the mob attacking the carnival -- immediate action, moral choice, multiple resolution paths. That is a textbook strong start.
- A strong start for Session 8 might be: “The sending stone you found in Room 4 crackles to life. A voice -- deep, resonant, like wind through a canyon -- says: ‘Zephyr has not reported. I am coming to see for myself.’ The stone goes dead. You have maybe an hour.”

### Step 3: Outline Potential Scenes (10 minutes)

**Do this most sessions.**

List 3-5 scenes that **might** happen. Not a sequence. Not a script. A menu of possibilities. Some will happen, some will not, and that is fine.

Write each scene as a single line:

- **What is happening** (not “what will happen”)
- **Who is there**
- **What makes it interesting** (conflict, discovery, choice)

**Key principle:** Scenes are possibilities, not requirements. If the players go somewhere unexpected, you can move a scene’s *content* to the new *location*. The interesting NPC you prepped for the tavern can show up at the docks instead.

**For our campaign, a typical scene list might look like:**

1. Confrontation with Caliban at the Typhoon’s Rest (Caliban arrives, tense standoff, portal in cellar)
2. Rasha al-Nadir approaches the party with an alliance offer (conflict: capture vs. kill)
3. The Marquess summons the party to the Palace (information fishing, suspiciously generous)
4. Zephyr returns to the Sluiceweave and finds Pyra guarding it (offscreen consequence)
5. City guard Captain Doria Venn asks for help with elemental disturbances

### Step 4: Define Secrets and Clues (10 minutes)

**Do this every session. This is the highest-value prep step after reviewing characters.**

Write 10 secrets or clues -- pieces of information the players might discover. These are **not tied to specific locations or methods of discovery.** This is the critical distinction from traditional prep.

A secret/clue is a single sentence of information that:

- Advances the plot
- Reveals character backstory
- Connects seemingly unrelated threads
- Foreshadows future events
- Rewards investigation or curiosity

**The key rule:** Secrets are **mobile**. If you wrote “Caliban’s binding is weakening” as a secret, the players might learn it from:

- Renz’s ledger in the warehouse
- The Primordial inscription on the pillar
- Rasha al-Nadir in conversation
- A vision when touching the brass brazier
- Maris at the tavern mentioning “Mr. Stormcaller seems desperate lately”

Prepare the *information*. Decide *how they find it* at the table based on what they actually do.

**Ten Secrets for a typical session in our campaign:**

1. Caliban’s energy siphoning weakened the city’s defenses, making the tsunami far worse than it should have been.
2. U’kotoa created the barrier that saved the city, and is gaining worship because of it.
3. The Marquess is a warlock of U’kotoa (or connected to the cult).
4. Mara (warehouse guard) reports to someone at the Palace.
5. The ley line anchor in the Sluiceweave predates the Divergence and is part of the city’s magical infrastructure.
6. Caliban needs three things to break free: smuggled artifacts, siphoned magical energy, and a portal site.
7. Rasha al-Nadir is a djinn hunter from the City of Brass who wants Caliban alive.
8. The amber from the Kuo-Toa may attract attention from U’kotoa cultists.
9. Ada’s summoned entity could exploit Caliban’s portal if it opens.
10. Lucien’s power may originate from U’kotoa, whether he knows it or not.

### Step 5: Develop Fantastic Locations (5 minutes)

**Do this when new locations are likely.**

Describe locations with three elements:

1. **A name** that evokes atmosphere (you are already good at this -- “Typhoon’s Rest,” “the Confluence of Three Waters”)
2. **Three sensory details** (what you see, hear, smell -- your session notes consistently do this well)
3. **One interesting feature** that affects gameplay (tactical terrain, interactive element, environmental hazard)

For Nicodranas, keep a short list of locations you might need. You do not need maps for most of them -- a few bullet points suffice.

### Step 6: Outline Important NPCs (5 minutes)

**Do this when new NPCs are likely.**

For each NPC, note:

- **Name** (keep a list of pre-generated names for improvised NPCs)
- **One distinguishing trait** (visual, vocal, behavioral)
- **What they want** (motivation drives roleplay better than personality descriptions)

You already do detailed NPC work (Renz, Maris, Jack, etc.). The Lazy DM approach says: for **planned** NPCs, a sentence or two is enough. Save the detail for NPCs the players actually engage with, then flesh them out between sessions.

**NPC shorthand for our campaign:**

| NPC | Trait | Want |
|-----|-------|------|
| Renz Kallow | Ink-stained, anxious | Protect his family, escape |
| Maris Saltwind | Sharp eyes, arms crossed | Keep her business, survive |
| Rasha al-Nadir | Calm intensity, genasi features | Capture Caliban alive |
| Doria Venn | Exhausted, clipped speech | Maintain order with no resources |
| Constable Dave Davidson | Earnest, out of his depth | Do the right thing |

### Step 7: Choose Relevant Monsters (5 minutes)

**Do this when combat is likely.**

Do not build full encounters in advance. Instead, choose 3-5 monsters or stat blocks that fit the session’s themes, and have their stats accessible.

For this campaign’s current arc, keep handy:

- **Djinni** (MM 144, modified to 100 HP for Caliban)
- **Air Elemental** (MM 124)
- **Invisible Stalker** (MM 192)
- **Bandit Captain** (for Jack Rainer)
- **Thug** (for guards and smugglers)
- **Dust Mephit** (for ambush surprises)

Use the “one monster, many descriptions” approach: a Thug stat block can be a warehouse guard, a smuggler, a dockworker, or a street tough. The stat block is the mechanics; the fiction is flexible.

### Step 8: Select Magic Item Rewards (5 minutes)

**Do this occasionally, not every session.**

Tie rewards to the narrative. Items found should feel like they belong in the world, not like video game loot drops.

**For our campaign:** The censers, bottled elementals, Jack’s +2 rapier, the amber, the sending stone -- all of these are narratively embedded rewards. That is the right approach.

When choosing magic items, ask:

- Does this item fit a **character’s wish list** (bag of holding, bracers of defense, etc.)?
- Does this item create **interesting choices** (the bottled elementals are dangerous but valuable)?
- Does this item **connect to the story** (Caliban’s brass amulet)?

## Part 3: What NOT to Prep

This is just as important as what to prep. Stop doing these things:

### Do Not Script Outcomes

Your Session 7 notes have “Possible Endpoints” (low-key, medium, high-energy). That is good -- those are possibilities, not plans. Never decide in advance how the session ends. Let the players determine that.

### Do Not Over-Prep Combat Encounters

You need stat blocks, not tactical battle plans. Know what the enemies want and how they fight in general terms. The specifics will depend on what the players do.

### Do Not Write Dialogue

Note what an NPC **knows** and **wants**. The words will come at the table. Your Marquess dialogue examples are useful as *inspiration* but do not try to deliver them verbatim -- the players will take the conversation in directions you cannot predict.

### Do Not Build What They Will Not See

If you prep three locations and the party only visits one, that is not wasted prep -- those locations still exist and can appear later. But do not spend hours detailing a dungeon room-by-room when the party might negotiate, sneak, or bypass entirely.

### Do Not Plan Beyond the Next Session

Have a **direction**, not a **destination**. Know the big picture (U’kotoa plot, Matron’s body, Caliban’s scheme) but only detail what is immediately relevant. The one exception: if a reveal or twist needs groundwork laid multiple sessions in advance (like the Marquess’s true nature), note the foreshadowing you want to drop, not the scene where it pays off.

## Part 5: The Original vs. The Return -- How They Complement Each Other

### The Lazy Dungeon Master (2012) -- Core Concepts

The original book established the philosophical foundation:

1. **Focus on fun, not fidelity.** The game exists to create enjoyable experiences, not to simulate a world with perfect accuracy. When rules and fun conflict, fun wins.
2. **Know your players.** Understanding what your players enjoy (combat, roleplay, exploration, mystery) is more valuable than any amount of world-building. For our table: the players clearly enjoy investigation, character interaction, moral complexity, and combat with environmental features.
3. **Build situations, not stories.** Create NPCs with goals, locations with features, and conflicts with stakes. The “story” emerges from play. Your Caliban arc is a great example: you built a villain with a plan, three locations, and multiple factions with competing interests. The story is whatever the players make of it.
4. **Let the dice surprise you.** Do not fudge rolls or force outcomes. If the players roll brilliantly on investigation, give them more than you planned. If they fail, let failure create complications. Shin lighting the Lantern and a fire elemental melting the censers? That is the kind of moment that only happens when you are open to surprise.
5. **Use published material.** Do not reinvent the wheel. The Exandria setting, the Monster Manual, published adventures -- all of these are tools to reduce your prep burden. Reskin, adapt, borrow freely.
6. **Prepare to improvise.** Keep lists (names, locations, rumours, random encounters) that you can pull from when the players go off-script. The “I Know a Guy” house rule is a perfect example of structured improvisation.

### Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master (2018) -- Refined Framework

The sequel codified the approach into the **eight-step checklist** detailed in Part 2 above, and added several critical concepts:

1. **Secrets and Clues are the engine.** This is the book’s most important contribution. By decoupling information from specific discovery methods, you ensure that the players always have a path forward regardless of their choices. The information finds them, not the other way around.
2. **Spiral campaign development.** Start with broad strokes and add detail only where the players focus their attention. For our campaign: you know the big picture (U’kotoa, the Matron, the gods as mortals) but you only need to detail the immediate arc (Caliban) in depth. The rest can wait.
3. **Session zero is ongoing.** Check in with players regularly about what they enjoy, what they want to see more of, and what is not working. This is not a one-time event.
4. **The art of the strong start.** Opening with energy and immediacy sets the tone for the entire session. Your mob scene at the carnival is a textbook example.
5. **Embrace the gray.** Moral complexity makes better stories than clear good-versus-evil. Your campaign already does this brilliantly: the Marquess is Lucien’s mother AND a U’kotoa cultist. Dazz is helpful AND has his own agenda. Rasha wants justice AND it conflicts with the party’s mission. Keep leaning into this.
6. **Think in terms of “fronts.”** Multiple active threats that advance independently of the players create a living world. Your current fronts are:
   - **Caliban’s liberation scheme** (immediate)
   - **U’kotoa’s growing influence** (medium-term)
   - **The Matron’s missing body** (long-term, Alarise)
   - **Ada’s summoned entity** (long-term, ticking clock)
   - **The Marquess’s machinations** (medium-term, Lucien)

## Part 6: Session Prep in Practice -- Pacing and Timing

### The 30-Minute Prep

For a routine session where you are continuing an established arc:

| Step | Time | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Review characters | 5 min | Skim last session’s notes, note unresolved threads |
| Strong start | 5 min | One paragraph, one compelling opening |
| Scenes | 5 min | 3-5 bullet points |
| Secrets & clues | 10 min | Write 10 pieces of information |
| Quick NPC/monster check | 5 min | Confirm you have the stat blocks you need |

### The 60-Minute Prep

For a session that introduces a major new location, faction, or turning point:

Add to the above:

- Develop 1-2 new locations with sensory details (10 min)
- Create or flesh out 2-3 new NPCs (10 min)
- Choose and customize treasure/rewards (5 min)
- Review the big picture and note foreshadowing opportunities (5 min)

### Between-Session Work (Optional, As Inspiration Strikes)

This is not “prep” but “creative development.” Do it only when you are energized, not as an obligation:

- Write NPC backstory or dialogue you are excited about
- Develop world-building that excites you (like the Gods as Mortals doc)
- Draw maps or find art for locations
- Think about long-term plot threads
- Read source material (Critical Role wiki, rulebooks)

The key distinction: **session prep is an obligation you keep short. Creative development is a hobby you indulge when inspired.** Do not confuse them.

## Part 8: Campaign-Specific Advice

### Your Strengths (Keep Doing These)

Based on reviewing Sessions 1-7, you are already doing several things well:

- **Rich sensory descriptions.** Your location write-ups consistently include smell, sound, sight, and atmosphere. This is excellent and the players clearly benefit from it.
- **Multiple resolution paths.** You consistently offer social, stealth, and combat solutions. The mob scene had persuasion, intimidation, performance, and several “let it play out” options.
- **Character-connected hooks.** Each major plot element ties to at least one PC’s personal arc (Ada/ley lines, Alarise/Matron, Lucien/mother, Shin/faith).
- **Morally complex NPCs.** The Marquess, Dazz, Rasha, even Skrang -- none of these are simple good-or-evil. This creates rich roleplay.
- **Connected plot threads.** The Caliban/U’kotoa/Marquess web is excellent -- events in one thread ripple into others.

### Areas to Streamline

- **Combat stat blocks.** Your session docs include full stat block details inline. Consider keeping a separate quick-reference sheet for combat stats, and only noting the *narrative* aspects in your session prep (who fights, what they want, when they surrender).
- **Contingency planning.** Some of your docs plan for many possible outcomes (“If X then Y, if A then B”). Trust yourself to handle these at the table. Note the *situation* and the *NPC motivations*, and the outcomes will flow naturally.
- **Session length.** “Budget 3-4 hours of play” with three major locations plus multiple combat encounters is ambitious. The Lazy DM approach says: prep enough for 2-3 hours and let the players’ pace dictate what gets covered. Unused material carries forward.

## Part 9: The One-Page Session Prep

If you do nothing else, do this. Copy the template from Part 4 and fill in only the bolded sections. This takes 15-20 minutes and gives you everything you need for a solid session.

**Minimum viable prep:**

1. **Review characters** -- one sentence per PC about their current state
2. **Strong start** -- one paragraph opening scene
3. **Secrets & clues** -- at least 5, ideally 10
4. **Have your stat blocks ready** -- bookmark the pages or have the PDFs open

Everything else, you can handle at the table. You have proven that across seven sessions. Trust yourself.

## Part 10: At-the-Table Facilitation Techniques

*Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master*, Chapters 19-20, covers techniques that happen during play rather than in prep. These were absent from earlier versions of this guide.

### The Player-Led Session Recap

At the start of every session, **ask the players to summarize what happened last time.** Do not summarize it yourself.

- Say nothing while they talk. Just listen and take notes.
- Do not correct minor inaccuracies unless a misunderstanding would break the session.
- Only step in to clarify something genuinely vital to the upcoming action.

**Why this matters more than it seems:** What the players remember — and how they frame it — reveals what actually resonated. If they talk for three minutes about Sushi the dog and thirty seconds about the elemental, that tells you something about what to lean into. It also surfaces misunderstandings about plot before they become problems, and it re-engages the table at the start of the session without you doing all the work.

After they finish, you can add a brief “and here’s where we pick up” to set the scene.

### Three Tricks for Group Storytelling

These are small delegation techniques that make the narrative feel collaborative rather than DM-delivered.

**1. “Describe your killing blow.”**

When a player lands the finishing hit on a significant enemy, pause and ask: *”How do you want to do this?”* (or simply “Describe it.”) Let them narrate the moment entirely. Do not interrupt.

This costs you nothing and gives the player ownership of a memorable scene. It also shifts the table’s energy — a player who just described their character dramatically ending a fight is more invested than one who said “I roll 14 damage.”

**2. “What’s an interesting feature of this monster?”**

When the party encounters a creature they have not seen before, ask a player (ideally one who is waiting for their turn): *”What’s the first thing you notice about it?”*

This does three things: it helps identify creatures in theater-of-the-mind play, it distributes narration across the table, and it generates details you can use yourself. If a player says “it has no eyes,” lean in — “right, it’s tracking you entirely by sound.”

**3. “What happens along your journey?”**

When the party travels between locations, rather than summarizing it yourself, set the stage and then ask players to contribute:

*”You set off from the docks toward the Open Quay. The streets are still damp from last night’s rain. What happens on the way?”*

Point to a player. Let them describe a moment. This works especially well for montage sequences (a day of investigation, a week of downtime, an ocean voyage). It generates story without you having to invent it, and players often introduce details that become hooks.

**A note on using these selectively:** Do not use all three every session. They work best when deployed at moments with genuine dramatic weight. Killing blow narration means more when the enemy was a real threat. “What do you notice?” lands better for a memorable monster than for the fifth bandit of the night.
